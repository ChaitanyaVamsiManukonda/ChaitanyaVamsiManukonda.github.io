Energy Generation with Parabolic Thin Film Photovoltaic Modules by Integration of Stirling Engine

May 2021

Abstract

This project presents a novel approach to solar energy collection by combining parabolic concentrators with thin-film photovoltaic (PV) modules and Stirling engines. By capturing both direct solar radiation and its reflective losses, the setup aims to enhance the overall energy efficiency significantly. The system eschews traditional anti-reflective coatings to maintain the Stirling engine's operational efficacy, thereby maximizing the conversion of captured solar energy into electricity.

Detailed Description

The project leverages Copper Indium Gallium Selenide (CIGS) PV modules known for their high efficiency and cost-effectiveness, with a capability to reach efficiencies up to 22.6%. These modules are uniquely integrated with a parabolic concentrator that focuses solar radiation to a point where a Stirling engine can convert the accumulated thermal energy into mechanical work, subsequently transformed into electrical energy.

A critical innovation in this setup is the avoidance of anti-reflective coatings which, while beneficial to reducing reflective losses in traditional solar panels, can diminish the heat available for the Stirling engine. The design thus preserves the engine's thermal efficiency by allowing more radiation to reach the concentrator's focal point, where the engine is located.

The construction involves sector-shaped CIGS panels mounted on a durable parabolic aluminum frame designed to withstand outdoor environmental conditions. The panels' arrangement and the precise positioning of the Stirling engine at the parabolic dish's focus maximize the system's energy capture and conversion capabilities. A regenerator within the Stirling engine further enhances efficiency by recycling the heat.

Technical Aspects

PV Modules: Use of sector-shaped CIGS photovoltaics which allow for a flexible setup to focus the reflecting beam onto the stirling setup and achieve high conversion efficiency.

Parabolic Concentrator: Engineered to focus solar radiation effectively, increasing the temperature at the focal point where the Stirling engine operates.

Stirling Engine: Employs cyclic compression and expansion of air or other gases at varying temperatures to convert heat energy efficiently into mechanical work, enhanced by a regenerator that recycles heat.

System Design: Avoidance of anti-reflective coatings to maximize heat accumulation at the focal point, crucial for the optimal performance of the Stirling engine.

Conclusion

The integration of these components forms a cutting-edge system that not only addresses the efficiency limitations of conventional photovoltaic systems but also introduces a sustainable solution to meet the increasing energy demands. The project represents a significant step forward in the application of combined heat and power systems in renewable energy technologies.
